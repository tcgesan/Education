Grameen Bank



Grameen Bank is a famous microfinance institution in Bangladesh, founded by Nobel Peace Prize winner Dr. Muhammad Yunus in 1983. The main purpose of Grameen Bank is to help the poor, especially in rural areas, by offering small loans known as microcredit. Traditional banks usually require collateral, which many poor people do not have, so Grameen Bank created a new model that does not need collateral. This approach makes it easier for people with limited resources to borrow money, start small businesses, and improve their living conditions.

One unique feature of Grameen Bank is its focus on empowering women, who make up the majority of its borrowers. By providing women with financial support, the bank helps them contribute more to their families and gain independence. The success of Grameen Bank has also inspired many similar programs worldwide, proving that microfinance can be a powerful tool for reducing poverty. Today, Grameen Bank serves millions of people across Bangladesh, and it continues to play an important role in promoting economic development and social change. Through its innovative model, Grameen Bank has shown that even small loans can create big opportunities for people to improve their lives.
